Figures 3, 4, and 5 show a comparative analysis of feature orthogonality and robustness between GeoCMON (Residual method) and DIMON (Baseline) at different depth levels (Layer 1 to Layer 3). The heatmap on the left plots the feature correlation matrices of Branch ①, Branch ②, and Trunk subnetworks, respectively, demonstrating the impact of the two methods on feature representation. The Residual method significantly improves feature orthogonality through conditional residual connections, driving inter-feature correlation coefficients close to zero and reducing inter-modal interference. The bar charts on the right show the convergence error and error variance of each subnetwork. The results demonstrate that the GeoCMON method achieves lower convergence error and better error stability at all levels, demonstrating superior learning stability and representation capability.

The first row of the figure shows the pairwise correlation coefficients of the three feature dimensions (F1, F2, and F3) in each subnetwork (Branch1, Branch2, and Trunk) of the DIMON baseline model, revealing a mixture of highly negative and positive correlations. The second row shows the correlation coefficient matrix for the corresponding layers of GeoCMON's residual structure. The coloring tends to be uniform, indicating greater independence of each feature. The bar chart on the right compares the convergence error and error variance of the two methods. The purple bar represents GeoCMON, demonstrating its superior overall performance.

This set of figures fully demonstrates the feature decoupling effect achieved by GeoCMON through conditional residual connections. This facilitates the effective separation and stable fusion of features from different modalities in the multi-branch architecture, significantly improving the robustness and generalization of the learned operators.
